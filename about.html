We convinced ourselves that playing games requires time we don't have. A free evening. A friend available at the same moment. A dedicated hour. None of that is true.

The modern life problem

You have a job. A family. Errands. A never-ending list of things to do. By the time you sit down, it's late, you're tired, and the idea of scheduling a game night feels like one more thing to organize.

So you stop playing. Not because you don't want to. Because life doesn't leave room for it.

But you always have five minutes. At the coffee machine. Waiting for a meeting to start. On the bus. Before bed.

That's all it takes. One move. Send it. Go back to your life. Your rival will respond when they have their five minutes. And so it goes — a game that breathes with the rhythm of real life instead of fighting it.

This is how people used to play

Before the internet, players sent their moves by mail. A postcard with a chess move would cross continents, take weeks to arrive, and the game would still be alive months later. People had dozens of games going at once — each with a different person, each at its own pace.

Bobby Fischer played correspondence games with dozens of people simultaneously. You can too — without the stamps.

Nobody thought that was unusual. It was just how you played when you couldn't be in the same room.
